The Double Slit Experiment — A Walking Script

Estimated listening time: 30–36 minutes at 0.9x speed.

· · ·

Setting the Stage

Alright, let's talk about what might be the single most important experiment in all of physics. The double slit experiment. It's been around for over two hundred years, and it still messes with people's heads — including the heads of professional physicists.

Here's the setup. Imagine you're in a dark room. On one side, a laser. On the other, a white wall — the screen. In between, a barrier with two narrow vertical slits cut into it, side by side. That's it. Light source, barrier, screen.

The light from the laser is featureless — a smooth, uniform beam, the same everywhere. No structure, no pattern. And yet, on the screen behind the barrier, you see alternating vertical bands of bright and dark, like a barcode. Where did that structure come from? How does a featureless input produce a structured output?

· · ·

Part One — From Flat Wave to Two Sources

The laser sends what physicists call a plane wave — a flat, uniform sheet of light marching toward the barrier. Think of a marching band in perfect formation, shoulder to shoulder, all stepping forward together. The wave has no left-right bias. It's symmetric.

Now this wave hits the barrier. Most of it is blocked. But at the two slits, tiny slivers get through. And here's the crucial thing: the moment the wave squeezes through a narrow opening, it fans out on the other side. This is called diffraction. The narrower the opening, the more it spreads. Each slit becomes a new wave source, radiating outward into the space behind the barrier.

So now instead of one flat wave, you have two overlapping waves expanding from two points. Two sources, side by side. And when two waves overlap, they interfere — at some points they reinforce each other, at others they cancel. That interference is what creates the barcode pattern.

· · ·

Part Two — Path Difference and the Barcode

Here's the mechanism. Pick a point on the screen right in the center, exactly halfway between the two slits. The wave from the left slit and the wave from the right slit travel equal distances to reach that point. They arrive in sync. Peak meets peak. Bright spot.

Now pick a point a little to the right of center. It's closer to the right slit, farther from the left slit. The two path lengths differ. If that difference is exactly half a wavelength, the waves arrive perfectly out of sync — peak meets trough. They cancel. Dark spot.

A little further right, the path difference hits a full wavelength. Back in sync. Bright again. And so it goes — as you scan across the screen, you alternate between reinforcement and cancellation. Bright, dark, bright, dark. That's your barcode.

The bands run vertically because each slit is tall — the geometry is the same at every height, so each bright or dark zone extends from top to bottom of the screen. That's the whole explanation for the shape of the pattern. The interesting part is what comes next.

· · ·

Analogy — The Concert Speakers

If you've ever been at an outdoor concert with two speakers on stage, side by side, playing the same tone — you've experienced this. Walk sideways through the crowd, parallel to the stage. At some spots the music is loud and full. At other spots it goes weirdly quiet. Loud, quiet, loud, quiet. That's interference — the two speakers are like the two slits, and your sideways movement changes the path difference from each speaker to your ears.

· · ·

Part Three — One Photon at a Time

So far, this is just wave physics. Water does it. Sound does it. No surprises. The reason the double slit experiment blows minds is what happens when you turn the intensity way, way down.

Imagine sending just one photon at a time. One particle of light. It hits the screen somewhere — a single dot. You record where it lands. Then you send another. Another dot. Each one lands at a specific point, like a bullet hitting a target.

But after thousands of photons, you look at the accumulated dots. And it's the interference pattern. The barcode. The same pattern you get with a full wave. Each photon went through the slits alone. There was nothing to interfere with — except itself.

It's as if each photon went through both slits simultaneously, interfered with itself, and landed at a point determined by that self-interference. The photon acts like a wave when traveling and like a particle when it lands. That's the central mystery of quantum mechanics. Nobody can explain it in a way that feels intuitive. Richard Feynman famously said that nobody understands quantum mechanics. The math predicts the pattern perfectly, but the math doesn't come with a picture your brain can hold.

· · ·

Part Four — The Measurement Twist

Here's where it gets even stranger. Suppose you set up a detector at the slits to check which slit each photon actually goes through. You can do this with modern equipment. And when you do — when you know which slit the photon passed through — the interference pattern disappears. You just get two blobs, one behind each slit. The classic particle pattern.

The act of detecting which path the photon takes destroys the interference. It's not a limitation of our equipment. It's a fundamental feature of nature. If the which-path information exists anywhere in the universe — even if no human looks at it — the pattern goes away. The photon behaves like a particle when observed and like a wave when not observed.

Think of it this way. Imagine you have a magic coin that, when flipped in a dark room, somehow lands on edge every time. But the moment you shine a flashlight on it, it falls to heads or tails. The flashlight doesn't push it — it just reveals the state, and that revelation changes the outcome. That's a loose analogy, but it captures the flavor.

· · ·

Part Five — The Delayed Choice

Now here's where most explanations of the double slit stop. But there's a further twist that makes the whole thing even more unsettling, and it has to do with timing. The physicist John Wheeler asked a deceptively simple question: what if you wait to decide whether to measure the which-path information until after the photon has already passed through the slits?

Think about why this is strange. In the standard version, you might tell yourself a story: the detector at the slits disturbs the photon as it passes through, and that disturbance is what destroys the interference. It's not a great story — the math doesn't support it — but at least it's a story your intuition can hold onto. The photon arrives at the slit, gets jostled by the detector, and changes its behavior. Cause, then effect. Normal order.

Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment blows that up. Here's how it works. You let the photon pass through the slits with no detector. It sails through, undisturbed, and travels toward the screen. But before it arrives, you quickly insert or remove a device that determines whether you'll learn which path the photon took. The decision happens after the photon has already committed to going through the slits.

And the result? It doesn't matter when you decide. If the final setup reveals which-path information, you get the particle pattern — two blobs, no interference. If the final setup erases the which-path information, you get the wave pattern — interference bands. The photon's behavior at the slits — in the past — appears to depend on a choice you make in the future.

Let that settle for a moment. The photon has already passed through the slits. That event is over. And yet the pattern it contributes to on the screen depends on what you decide to do afterward. It's as if the photon retroactively decided to go through one slit or both, depending on whether you were going to check.

· · ·

Analogy — The Sealed Envelope

Here's a way to feel the strangeness. Imagine you're playing a game. A friend flips a coin and puts the result in a sealed envelope, then hands it to you. You walk across town with the envelope. At any point before you open it, your friend can call you and say either: I'm going to tell you what's in the envelope, or: I've forgotten what I put in. In a normal world, neither phone call changes what's already in the envelope. The coin was flipped. The result is fixed.

But in the quantum version, the phone call changes the result. If your friend says they'll reveal the information, you open the envelope and find heads or tails — a definite outcome. If they say the information is lost, you open the envelope and find the coin balancing on its edge — an outcome that shouldn't be possible. And here's the kicker: the coin was sealed in the envelope before the phone call happened. The decision your friend made after the fact changed what you find inside.

That's the delayed-choice experiment. The photon is the coin. The envelope is the slit. The phone call is your decision, made later, about whether to measure. And somehow, the later decision shapes the earlier result.

· · ·

Quick Detour — What Is Polarization?

Before we go further, we need one more concept: polarization. It'll matter in a moment.

Light is a wave, and like all waves it oscillates — it vibrates. But unlike sound, which vibrates in the same direction it's traveling, light vibrates sideways, perpendicular to its direction of travel. Polarization is just the direction of that sideways vibration. A photon traveling toward you might be vibrating up and down — that's vertical polarization. Or left and right — horizontal polarization. Or at any angle in between. There's also circular polarization, where the direction of vibration rotates as the wave moves forward, like a corkscrew.

Here's the key thing for our purposes: you can filter polarization. A polarizing filter — the same kind used in sunglasses — only lets through light vibrating in one particular direction. Everything else gets blocked. And you can set a filter at any angle you want. So if you put a vertical filter over one slit and a horizontal filter over the other, every photon that comes through carries a tag: its polarization tells you which slit it came from. That tagging is about to become very important.

· · ·

Part Six — The Quantum Eraser

There's one more layer, and it's the most mind-bending of all. It's called the quantum eraser, and it was first demonstrated experimentally in the late 1990s.

Here's the setup. You do the double slit experiment, but you put a vertical polarizing filter over the left slit and a horizontal one over the right slit. Now every photon that comes through is tagged — its polarization tells you which slit it used. And with that tagging in place, the interference pattern disappears. The which-path information exists, so the photon behaves like a particle.

But now comes the trick. After the photon has already hit the screen and been detected — its position recorded, the event over and done with — you take the tag information and erase it. You pass the polarization through a diagonal filter, set at forty-five degrees, which scrambles vertical and horizontal into the same state. You can no longer tell which slit the photon came from. The which-path information is destroyed.

Now you look at the data. All the photon positions on the screen, taken together, still look like a blur — no interference. But here's the twist: if you sort the data based on which photons had their tags erased versus which ones didn't, a pattern emerges. The subset of photons whose tags were erased show the interference pattern. The subset whose tags survived show the particle pattern. The same photons, already detected, retroactively sort themselves into wave-like or particle-like behavior depending on what happened to their tags afterward.

This is not science fiction. It's been done in labs repeatedly. The results are solid. And they force a conclusion that our everyday language barely has the tools to express: the distinction between wave and particle isn't a property of the photon — it's a property of what information is available about the photon. Destroy the information, and the wave behavior reappears. Preserve it, and the particle behavior holds. And the timing doesn't matter. What happens to the information in the future can determine which pattern the photons form in the past.

· · ·

Analogy — The Photo Album

Here's one final analogy for the quantum eraser. Imagine a photographer takes pictures of people at a party. Each photo is tagged with the person's name. If you look at the photos sorted by name, you see clear groups — all the pictures of Alice, then all the pictures of Bob. Structure. Particle behavior. Now imagine someone goes through and rips the name tags off half the photos — the tags are destroyed, gone forever. Those untagged photos, when you flip through them, now seem randomly mixed, but if you look closely, they form a pattern based on where people were standing — clusters by location, by the geometry of the room. The interference pattern emerges from the untagged data. The tagged photos still sort by name. Same photos. Different patterns. Depending entirely on whether the tag survived.

The difference between the analogy and reality is that in the analogy, the pattern was always there — you just couldn't see it because the tags distracted you. In quantum mechanics, the pattern genuinely wasn't there until the tags were erased. The act of erasing the information brings the interference into existence — retroactively, for photons that were already detected.

· · ·

Part Seven — It's About the Information

Let's stay with this idea for a moment, because it might be the single most important insight to come out of quantum mechanics — and it's one that most popular explanations rush past.

When you first hear about the double slit, the natural reaction is to think the experiment is about photons. What are photons really? Are they waves or particles? What are they doing when we're not looking? These feel like the obvious questions. But after a century of experiments — the delayed choice, the quantum eraser, and dozens of variations — the lesson that keeps coming back is: the experiment isn't about the photon. It's about the information.

Think about what actually determines the pattern on the screen. It's not what the photon does at the slits. It's not whether a human looks. It's not the physical mechanism of the detector. The only thing that matters is whether which-path information exists — anywhere, in any form, recoverable by any means. If the information exists, you get particle behavior. If it doesn't, you get wave behavior. If it existed but was destroyed, wave behavior comes back. The photon itself doesn't change. What changes is the informational context surrounding it.

The physicist John Archibald Wheeler — the same one who proposed the delayed-choice experiment — captured this with a phrase: it from bit. The idea is that the physical world — the it — doesn't come first. Information — the bit — comes first. The properties of a physical system aren't fixed features sitting there waiting to be discovered. They're defined by the information that's available about the system. Before the information exists, the property doesn't have a definite value. It's not that we don't know which slit the photon went through. It's that the photon didn't go through one slit. The question didn't have an answer until the information was created.

This is genuinely hard to absorb, because our entire everyday experience is built on the opposite assumption. You assume the cup on your desk is there whether you look at it or not. You assume the coin in your pocket is heads or tails before you check. And for objects at human scale, that assumption works perfectly — quantum effects are so small they're undetectable. But at the photon level, the assumption breaks down. The cup on your desk is only well-defined because it's constantly interacting with air molecules, light, the table surface — all of which carry information about it. Strip away those interactions, isolate a single photon, and the definiteness evaporates. The photon isn't a little ball with a hidden trajectory. It's a cloud of possibilities that only sharpens into a definite outcome when information is created.

And the quantum eraser adds the final twist: that sharpening isn't permanent. Destroy the information, and the cloud of possibilities reasserts itself. Retroactively. For events that already happened. The past isn't fixed until the information about it is locked in.

This is why many physicists now talk about quantum mechanics not as a theory of particles or waves, but as a theory of information. The wave function isn't a physical wave rippling through space — it's a mathematical description of what we can and can't know. The collapse of the wave function isn't a physical event — it's an update to the available information. And interference isn't two physical waves adding up — it's what happens when no information exists to distinguish between two possibilities. The possibilities coexist, and their coexistence is what creates the pattern.

That's the deep takeaway from the double slit. The universe doesn't have hidden answers waiting to be uncovered. At the quantum level, the answers are created by the act of asking — and they can be uncreated by erasing the question.

Tying It Together

So what did we learn? The double slit experiment starts simple — two overlapping waves, path differences, bright and dark bands. Classical wave physics. But then you turn the intensity down and discover that individual photons, going through the slits one at a time, build up the same interference pattern. Each one appears to go through both slits at once.

Measure which slit — the pattern vanishes. Delay the measurement until after the photon has passed through — it still vanishes. Erase the measurement after the photon has already landed — the pattern comes back for the erased subset. The behavior of the photon in the past depends on what happens to information in the future.

The double slit experiment is a two-hundred-year-old setup that continues to be the cleanest demonstration that reality at the quantum level does not work the way our intuition says it should. Light is not a wave. Light is not a particle. It's something our language doesn't have a word for. And the distinction between wave and particle isn't a property of the photon — it's a property of what information exists about the photon. The double slit is the experiment that forces us to confront all of this.
